What Changed Since the Poster

Briefing for Ariya, April 9, 2026

From the AIX poster (March 2026) to the Nature Collection manuscript (April 2026)

The short version: We completely rebuilt the analysis pipeline. The data got bigger, the methods got stronger, and the core finding got even clearer. The main story is the same as the poster, but the numbers, the statistical backing, and the framing around "which themes are most compressed" all improved significantly.

Our Three Hypotheses (Updated)

H1: Semantic Compression
Artist frames will concentrate in a small subset of public discourse topics.
SUPPORTED: 99.9% of artist concerns land in just 4 of 20 topics.
H2: Semantic, Not Stylistic
Distributional divergence between artist frames and public discourse will persist after style controls.
SUPPORTED: Divergence persists. Format differences explain a modest portion of the gap. The rest is about substance.
H3: Multi-Level Compression (reframed)
Compression in public discourse operates at four distinct levels: topical exclusion, frame redirection, binary simplification, and voice collapse.
SUPPORTED. Each mechanism leaves a distinct geometric signature, and the four compound rather than substitute for each other.

What changed about H3: The poster framed this as "governance concerns are more compressed than emotional concerns." The new data shows something more interesting. Compression does not follow a clean governance-versus-emotional divide. It operates at four levels at once:

  1. Topical exclusion: 15 of 20 clusters contain no artist probes. Six of them carry artist-themed labels (532 chunks total) and still have zero artist voice.
  2. Frame redirection: All 252 ownership probes and all 252 utility probes flow into a single cluster pre-framed as "AI Art Authenticity and Human Creativity," reframing concrete property and labor positions as a debate about aesthetic philosophy.
  3. Binary simplification: The 251 threat probes split into exactly two clusters whose labels invert plain readings: "AI is a threat" lands in "AI as Creative Collaborator," and "AI is not a threat" lands in the authenticity cluster.
  4. Voice collapse: All three utility positions (positive, negative, unsure) end up in the same cluster, grouped by first-person practice register rather than by substantive content.

What Artists Actually Say (Example Frames)

These are real positions from the 252 artists in our survey. The point of showing them is that artists are not a monolithic group. They hold specific, sometimes contradictory positions that public discourse flattens.

The Pragmatic Dual-Holder

Views AI as both a threat AND a useful tool. Supports mandatory disclosure. Believes the original artist should own AI-generated work in their style. Would donate their work freely as training data.

This person views AI as dangerous but also sees its value, wants transparency, and would still contribute. Public discourse would file them under "backlash" and miss the complexity.

Style vs Substance (H2)

A key question is whether the gap between artists and public discourse is just about writing style (surveys vs news articles) or about actual content. We tested this by extracting short sentences from public documents that match the format of the survey statements.

What this means: Format differences account for a modest portion of the divergence (JSD drops 15%). But the association between where you come from (artist vs public) and which topics you land in stays strong. The divergence is predominantly about substance. Even when public discourse is chopped into short sentences that match the survey format, it does not converge with what artists are saying.

The headline number: 24:1. Artists hold 24 completely different positions on ownership, from "the artist should own everything" to "nobody should own AI art" to "it depends on the level of human involvement." All 24 collapse into a single public discourse topic. The public conversation registers "artists care about ownership" but erases the fact that artists disagree with each other about it in policy-relevant ways.

The destination matters as much as the count. Both ownership and utility get routed into one cluster labeled "AI Art Authenticity and Human Creativity." The label points to a philosophical debate about whether AI-generated work qualifies as art. The contents include every concrete property claim and every utility position artists make. The frame preserves the words but loses the demand. This is what "frame redirection" means in the H3 reframing.

Free-Text Validation: Do Templates Match Real Speech?

A potential criticism of our approach is that we use survey-template probes ("I agree that...") rather than how artists naturally speak. To test this, we took 38 free-text responses from the same Lovato survey where artists wrote in their own words about compensation.

84%
of free-text responses land in the same cluster region as the template probes
38
organic free-text responses tested

What this means: When artists write in their own words (not choosing from survey options), their language still lands in the same part of the semantic map as our template probes. The templates capture genuine artist language, not just survey-format artifacts. This is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that our probe construction is valid.

LLM Theme Validation

We had two independent AI models (GPT-5.4-mini from OpenAI and Claude Sonnet 4.6 from Anthropic) classify each of our 750 public probes by theme. Neither model knew how we had assigned the themes.

Both LLMs pick same theme

Why isn't accuracy 90%? Public discourse sentences are often multi-thematic. A sentence about "requiring companies to catalog training data provenance" touches transparency, ownership, and compensation simultaneously. The LLMs pick whichever theme seems most prominent, while our extraction picks by semantic similarity. The two approaches will disagree on genuinely ambiguous sentences. The kappa of 0.639 (substantial) is the key metric: it means the two LLMs agree with each other more than chance, confirming the probes are genuinely thematic.

What Did NOT Change

  • Artists are present in the conversation but compressed into a tiny corner
  • Most of the public AI-art discourse has no artist perspective at all
  • The gap is about substance, not just writing style
  • Ownership is the most dramatically compressed theme
  • 252 artists, 5 concern dimensions, same Lovato et al. survey data
  • This matters for governance: policymakers reading the news get a distorted picture

The poster presentation is accurate in its claims. The manuscript has stronger evidence, more precise numbers, and better methods, but the core story you presented at AIX is the same.
